Cultivating Comprehension

A Professional Development Resource for Supporting Tier 2 & Tier 3 Readers in Grades 4 & 5

Welcome, Educators!

This portfolio is designed to provide you with practical, evidence-based strategies to support students who require targeted reading instruction. By focusing on foundational comprehension skills, we can help every child blossom into a confident, capable reader.

Our Needs & Vision

To become active readers, students must first learn to use comprehension strategies. This professional development addresses this need by focusing on explicit, small-group instruction for Tier 2 and Tier 3 students.

Research shows that individualized, small-group instruction is highly effective for addressing specific skill gaps in comprehension (Al Otaiba et al., 2011). Our goal is to unify our school's approach to RTI by providing a common framework and language for teaching these strategies. Through modeling, hands-on practice, and collaboration (Beers, 2003; Shearer et al., 2019), we will create consistent and effective comprehension support for all students.

Key Comprehension Strategies

Activating Prior Knowledge

Help students connect what they already know to what they are reading. This builds a foundation for new information and helps them make connections between new information and what they already know.

Small Group Focus:

  • Tier 2: Use an "Anticipation Guide" where students agree/disagree with statements about the topic before reading. Also, try "Text-to-Self" connections using sentence starters like, "This reminds me of a time when..."
  • Tier 3: Do a "Concept Sort" with pictures or vocabulary words. Before reading a book about animals, have them sort pictures into "lives in water" and "lives on land." Also, use short, related video clips to build background knowledge.
‚ùì

Questioning

This strategy creates a reason for reading. Students ask questions to clarify meaning, understand characters and events more clearly, understand the author‚Äôs purpose, and make predictions. This creates a fluid conversation between the student and the text.

Small Group Focus:

  • Tier 2: Introduce "Question-Answer Relationship (QAR)." Help students identify if the answer is "Right There," "Think and Search," "Author and Me," or "On My Own." Use a "Parking Lot" chart where students can post questions on sticky notes as they read.
  • Tier 3: Use a "Question Spinner" with simple question words (Who, What, Where, When). Students spin and ask a question about a picture or a single sentence. Model turning statements from the text into questions.
üé¨

Visualizing

Also called creating "mind pictures," this strategy encourages readers to use their imagination and sensory details from the text to construct mental images. This active engagement with the text helps improve comprehension and memory.

Small Group Focus:

  • Tier 2: Do a "Guided Imagery" activity. Read a highly descriptive passage aloud while students have their eyes closed. Pause and have them "draw what they hear" on a whiteboard.
  • Tier 3: Use manipulatives like play-doh or pipe cleaners. After reading a simple sentence, have students build what they visualize. Also, have students act out a simple scene from the text.
üß©

Making Inferences

Inferencing is reaching a conclusion based on evidence and reasoning. It's a vital skill for higher-order thinking. This strategy helps students use their knowledge to identify implied ideas and become aware of their own thinking process.

Small Group Focus:

  • Tier 2: Play "Riddle Me This." Provide short riddles where students must infer the answer based on the clues. Use cartoon strips with the dialogue removed and have students infer what the characters are saying.
  • Tier 3: Be "Picture Detectives." Show a photograph of a person and ask students to infer their feelings based on facial expression and body language. Use wordless picture books to practice telling the story based on visual clues.
üéØ

Summarizing

This strategy moves beyond simple retelling. It focuses on students identifying and retelling the most important aspects of the story in order. Students learn to focus on key words, shorten the story into its main concepts, and consolidate the information in their own words.

Small Group Focus:

  • Tier 2: Use the "Somebody Wanted But So Then" (SWBST) framework to break down a narrative. Also, try the "GIST" strategy where students summarize a paragraph in exactly 15-20 words.
  • Tier 3: Create a "Story Rope." After reading, use a piece of yarn and have students tie a knot for each key event. They then use the knots as prompts to retell the story in sequence. Use picture sequencing cards for retelling.
üîë

Determining Importance

Help students sift through information to focus on what's most important, ignoring irrelevant details. This involves looking for text features, noticing repeated ideas, and thinking about the author's purpose.

Small Group Focus:

  • Tier 2: Have students become "Headline Writers." After reading a short non-fiction passage, they must write a newspaper-style headline that captures the main idea. Use two different colored highlighters to mark "main ideas" vs. "interesting details."
  • Tier 3: Use a "Must-Know vs. Nice-to-Know" T-chart with pre-written sentence strips for sorting. Focus on identifying text features like titles, headings, and bold words as clues to what is important.
üîÆ

Predicting

Effective readers use pictures, titles, headings, and personal experiences to make predictions before they even begin to read. This strategy involves thinking ahead, anticipating information and events, and then refining, revising, and verifying those predictions as they read.

Small Group Focus:

  • Tier 2: Do a "Mystery Bag" prediction. Place objects related to the story in a bag and have students revise their prediction as each object is revealed. Use a "Prediction Tracker" graphic organizer.
  • Tier 3: Use "Cover Clues." Before opening a book, have students make one simple prediction based only on the title and cover. Pause after the first page and ask, "Was your prediction on the right track?"
üí¨

Guided Discussions

This involves leading a conversation guided by a specific goal or idea to enhance understanding. This strategy is particularly useful when students are missing key concepts that are vital for comprehension. Using open-ended questions helps students explore their thinking.

Small Group Focus:

  • Tier 2: Use "Talking Sticks" or a "Talking Ball" to ensure equitable participation. Introduce "Accountable Talk" sentence stems like "I agree with... because..." or "I want to add on to what... said."
  • Tier 3: Implement "Think-Pair-Share." Ask a very specific, literal question. Give students a moment to think, then have them share their answer with a partner before sharing with the group. Use picture prompts to start conversations.

Guided Reading Questions

Use these questions to spark discussion and deepen comprehension before, during, and after reading.

Before Reading

  • Based on the title/cover, what do you think this story will be about?
  • What does the title remind you of?
  • What predictions can you make about the main character?
  • What are you wondering about?

During Reading

  • How do you think this text will end? Why?
  • What important events have already happened?
  • How do you think the main character will change?
  • Visualize an event from the story and describe it.
  • Is there anything you don't understand that we should reread?

After Reading

  • What do you think the author wanted you to learn?
  • Who does the main character remind you of?
  • How might the plot change if it was written from a different character's point of view?
  • Summarize this text in 2-4 sentences.
  • Would you read this text again? Why or why not?

Before Reading

  • What do you think the main topic of this text will be?
  • What do you already know about this topic?
  • What does the title or book cover remind you of?
  • What clues did the author give you through the title, images, or text features?

During Reading

  • Based on this heading, what do you think this section will be about?
  • How do the text features on this page relate to the text?
  • What have you learned so far?
  • What unfamiliar words has the author used in the text?
  • Summarize what we have read so far.

After Reading

  • What was this text mainly about?
  • What details from the text supported the main idea?
  • What is something new you learned from the text?
  • What questions do you still have about this topic?
  • Where could you go to find out more information?

The SMART Self-Monitoring Approach

Empower students to become metacognitive readers with the Self-Monitoring Approach to Reading and Thinking (SMART). This teaches them to monitor their own comprehension and use "fix-up" strategies when meaning breaks down.

Implementing the SMART Strategy

Phase 1: Teacher Modeling

The teacher should model the entire SMART strategy several times before asking students to use it. Use a think-aloud process to show students how you monitor your own reading, identify confusion, and use strategies to fix it.

Phase 2: Guided Practice

  1. 1
    Check for Understanding

    After students read a section, ask them to use a pencil to place a check mark (‚úì) next to each paragraph they understand and a question mark (?) by each one they do not.

  2. 2
    Stop and Paraphrase

    At the end of each paragraph, students stop and explain to themselves in their own words what they read. They may look back at the text as they do this.

  3. 3
    Reread and Identify the Problem

    When students finish, they go back to each question mark (?) and reread the paragraph. If they still don't understand, they should try to determine what the problem is: Is it an unfamiliar word? A confusing sentence? A new topic?

  4. 4
    Try a Repair Strategy

    Guide students to use a fix-up strategy: use the glossary, look at pictures or graphics, examine other parts of the chapter, or get help from the teacher or a classmate.

Phase 3: Independent Practice

Have students read a passage independently using the check mark/question mark system. They can then work with a partner, verbalizing what they understand and working together to solve problems. Emphasize that before asking for help, students should be able to identify the source of their problem and what they have already tried to do to solve it.

Example Lesson Plans

Unlocking the Clues: Making Inferences

4th Grade ELA

Objectives

  • Define inference and its role in comprehension.
  • Use textual evidence and background knowledge to make inferences.
  • Support inferences with evidence from the text.
  • Develop claims using evidence from multiple sources.

Materials

  • Age-appropriate informational or narrative texts
  • "Clues + What I Know = Inference" graphic organizer
  • Highlighters, whiteboard, sentence frames
  • "Think-Aloud" prompt cards

5E Model Procedure

1. Engage (10-15 min)

Mystery Bag/Picture: Use a "mystery bag" or an evocative picture to have students guess what's inside or what's happening. Explain this "figuring out" is making an inference.

2. Explore (15-20 min)

Shared Reading & Clue Hunt: Read a short, engaging text aloud. As a class, highlight or underline specific words and phrases that serve as clues.

3. Explain (20-25 min)

Explicit Instruction: Introduce the formula: Text Clues + What I Already Know = Inference. Use a think-aloud to model filling out the graphic organizer with an example from the text.

4. Elaborate (25-30 min)

Partner Practice (Think-Pair-Share): In pairs, students use a new passage and the graphic organizer to answer inferential questions, using prompt cards to articulate their thinking.

5. Evaluate (15-20 min)

Independent Application: Students independently use the graphic organizer and write a short paragraph to answer an inferential question about a new text, supporting their claim with evidence.

Differentiation & Assessment

Differentiation Strategies
  • Pre-teach vocabulary and chunk text for struggling readers.
  • Provide sentence frames and visual supports for ELs.
  • Offer extended time and reduced workload for students with IEPs.
  • Challenge advanced learners with complex texts and have them create their own inferential questions.
Assessment Methods
  • Formative: Observe partner discussions and review graphic organizers during class.
  • Summative: Evaluate the independent written response for a clear inference supported by textual evidence.

Summarizing with "Somebody Wanted But So Then"

5th Grade ELA - Summarizing Narrative Texts

Objectives

  • Differentiate between retelling and summarizing.
  • Identify essential narrative elements (Somebody, Wanted, But, So, Then).
  • Construct a three-sentence summary paragraph.

Materials

  • Short narrative texts
  • "Somebody Wanted But So Then" graphic organizer
  • Chart paper or whiteboard
  • Optional: Sentence starters for summarizing

5E Model Procedure

1. Engage (10-15 min)

Activate Prior Knowledge: Ask students to "tell you about" a recently read story. Guide them to notice the difference between retelling every detail versus focusing on the main points, introducing the concept of summarizing.

2. Explore (15-20 min)

Introduce Strategy: Introduce the "Somebody Wanted But So Then" framework as a tool to find the essential parts of a story. Explain what each part represents (main character, goal, problem, action, resolution).

3. Explain (20-25 min)

Teacher Modeling: Read a short, familiar text aloud. Use a think-aloud to fill out the "SWBST" organizer on chart paper. Then, model how to combine these elements into a concise 3-sentence summary paragraph.

4. Elaborate (25-30 min)

Partner Practice: In pairs, students read a new short text and collaboratively complete the "SWBST" organizer. They then work together to draft their 3-sentence summary.

5. Evaluate (10-15 min)

Independent Application: Students independently read a new text, complete the organizer, and write their own summary. Collect these to assess their application of the strategy.

Differentiation & Assessment

Differentiation Strategies
  • Provide partially filled graphic organizers for Tier 2/3 students.
  • Use picture sequencing cards for ELs to build the narrative first.
  • Offer sentence frames for constructing the summary paragraph.
  • Challenge advanced learners to summarize a chapter or longer text.
Assessment Methods
  • Formative: Review completed "SWBST" organizers for accuracy in identifying key story elements.
  • Summative: Evaluate the independently written summary paragraph using a rubric for conciseness and inclusion of essential elements.

Crystal Ball Reading: Making Predictions

4th/5th Grade ELA

Objectives

  • Define prediction and its role in active reading.
  • Use text features to make predictions before reading.
  • Use textual evidence to make and refine predictions during reading.
  • Verify or adjust predictions and explain reasoning.

Materials

  • Age-appropriate texts with clear features
  • "Prediction Tracker" graphic organizer
  • Highlighters or sticky notes
  • Sentence frames for predictions

5E Model Procedure

1. Engage (10-15 min)

Mystery Cover/Title: Show only the cover or title of a new text. Ask students to predict what it will be about and why. Explain this is making a prediction, an educated guess based on clues.

2. Explore (15-20 min)

Text Feature Walk: Guide students to examine all text features (headings, illustrations, etc.) and record their initial predictions on the "Prediction Tracker" graphic organizer before reading.

3. Explain (20-25 min)

Model "Abandon/Enhance": Read the first section of the text. Use a think-aloud to model how new information confirms, changes ("enhances"), or disproves ("abandons") your initial prediction. Introduce sentence frames like "I think..." or "If..., then...".

4. Elaborate (25-30 min)

Guided Practice with Checkpoints: Continue reading, pausing at strategic points. In pairs, have students discuss new evidence and decide whether to abandon or enhance their predictions on their organizers.

5. Evaluate (15-20 min)

Independent Application: Students use a new short passage to independently complete the entire "Prediction Tracker," including a final reflection on whether their prediction was correct and why.

Differentiation & Assessment

Differentiation Strategies
  • Chunk text into smaller sections for making predictions.
  • Provide sentence frames for both oral and written predictions.
  • Use visuals and audio support for ELs and students with IEPs.
  • Challenge advanced learners to justify multiple plausible predictions.
Assessment Methods
  • Formative: Observe partner discussions and check "Prediction Tracker" organizers at checkpoints.
  • Summative: Evaluate the completed independent "Prediction Tracker" and written reflection for logical refinement of predictions based on evidence.

The Power of Questions: Unlocking Text Comprehension

4th/5th Grade ELA

Objectives

  • Differentiate between literal, inferential, and evaluative questions.
  • Generate questions before, during, and after reading.
  • Explain how questioning enhances comprehension.

Materials

  • Age-appropriate text
  • "Questioning Cube" or question word cards
  • "My Questioning Toolkit" graphic organizer
  • Sentence frames for question generation

5E Model Procedure

1. Engage (10-15 min)

Question Scavenger Hunt: Start with a "Mystery Object" and have students generate as many questions as they can about it. Explain that good readers do the same with texts.

2. Explore (15-20 min)

Text Preview: Before reading, guide students to look at text features and generate 2-3 "Before Reading" questions on their graphic organizer.

3. Explain (20-25 min)

Explicit Instruction: Define and model the three types of questions: Literal (On the Line), Inferential (Between the Lines), and Evaluative (Beyond the Lines), using think-alouds with the text.

4. Elaborate (25-30 min)

Collaborative Questioning: In pairs, students read the text in chunks and generate at least one of each question type, categorizing them on their graphic organizers.

5. Evaluate (15-20 min)

Independent Application: With a new text, students independently generate and categorize questions for before, during, and after reading, then reflect on how the process helped them understand.

Differentiation & Assessment

Differentiation Strategies
  • Provide question stems for each question type.
  • Use visual cues (e.g., hand gestures) to represent each type.
  • Conduct the lesson in intensive small groups for Tier 2/3 students.
  • Challenge advanced learners to create questions for their peers to answer.
Assessment Methods
  • Formative: Observe partner discussions and review "My Questioning Toolkit" organizers.
  • Summative: Evaluate the independently generated questions for variety, appropriateness, and correct categorization.

Painting Pictures with Words: Visualizing as We Read

4th/5th Grade ELA

Objectives

  • Define visualizing as creating mental images.
  • Use textual clues (sensory details) to create mental images.
  • Explain how visualizing enhances comprehension.

Materials

  • Descriptive narrative or non-fiction text
  • Art supplies or digital drawing tools
  • "My Mental Movie" graphic organizer
  • Sentence frames for describing images

5E Model Procedure

1. Engage (10-15 min)

Sensory Warm-up: Ask students to close their eyes and imagine a familiar place, describing what they see, hear, and smell. Explain this is what good readers do with text.

2. Explore (15-20 min)

Blind Reading & Sharing: Read a highly descriptive passage aloud while students close their eyes. Afterward, have them sketch or verbally describe the "mental movie" they created.

3. Explain (20-25 min)

Teacher Think-Aloud: Model how specific words (adjectives, sensory details) help you create a mental picture. Demonstrate filling out the "My Mental Movie" graphic organizer.

4. Elaborate (25-30 min)

Guided Practice: Read a new passage in chunks. After each chunk, have students identify text clues and sketch or describe their mental image on their graphic organizer, refining it as they get more information.

5. Evaluate (15-20 min)

Independent Application: Students independently read a new passage and complete the "My Mental Movie" graphic organizer, then write a short reflection on how the strategy helped them.

Differentiation & Assessment

Differentiation Strategies
  • Chunk text into very small, manageable sections.
  • Allow drawing as the primary method of expression for students who struggle with writing.
  • Use real pictures or video clips to build background knowledge.
  • Challenge advanced learners to write their own descriptive passages.
Assessment Methods
  • Formative: Observe partner discussions and review "My Mental Movie" organizers for the connection between text and image.
  • Summative: Evaluate the independent graphic organizer and reflection for detailed, text-supported mental images.

Unpacking the Text: Finding the Main Idea

4th/5th Grade ELA

Objectives

  • Define main idea and supporting details.
  • Distinguish between essential and non-essential information.
  • Identify the main idea and key supporting details in a text.

Materials

  • Informational or narrative texts
  • "Main Idea and Supporting Details" graphic organizer
  • Highlighters or sticky notes
  • Sentence frames for summarizing

5E Model Procedure

1. Engage (10-15 min)

"Clutter" Activity: Show a busy picture and ask students to describe what it's "mostly about" in one sentence. Explain that reading is similar‚Äîwe find the most important part.

2. Explore (15-20 min)

Initial Read & Guess: Have students read a short text and share their first impressions of what it was mostly about. Record ideas without correction.

3. Explain (20-25 min)

Explicit Instruction: Define "Main Idea" (the most important point) and "Supporting Details" (facts/examples that prove it). Model finding these in a text using a think-aloud and filling out a graphic organizer.

4. Elaborate (25-30 min)

Guided Practice: In pairs, students read a new text, discuss the main idea, identify key details, and complete a graphic organizer together.

5. Evaluate (15-20 min)

Independent Application: Students independently read a new passage, complete a main idea graphic organizer, and write a one-sentence summary.

Differentiation & Assessment

Differentiation Strategies
  • Provide partially filled graphic organizers or sentence frames.
  • Guide students to highlight potential main ideas and details before writing.
  • Use the "SWBST" organizer for narrative texts.
  • Challenge advanced learners with texts where the main idea is implied.
Assessment Methods
  • Formative: Observe partner discussions and review graphic organizers for accurate identification of main idea and details.
  • Summative: Evaluate the independent graphic organizer and one-sentence summary for accuracy and conciseness.

High-Interest, Low-Readability Books

These books are written at a lower reading level but feature age-appropriate topics. They allow struggling readers to engage with complex themes without being frustrated by text complexity.

Examples: Orca Book Publishers, Saddleback Educational Publishing

Digital Reading Platforms

Platforms like Epic!, ReadWorks, and Newsela offer vast libraries of digital books with features like read-aloud support, in-text vocabulary definitions, and adjustable reading levels for scaffolding.

Graphic Novels

The combination of text and illustrations provides strong contextual support. They can help students practice inferencing and visualizing while keeping them highly engaged.

Popular Series: "Dog Man", "Smile", "Amulet"

Audiobooks & Paired Reading

Listening to a fluent reader models prosody and helps with decoding. Students can follow along in a physical book while listening to an audiobook to build fluency and confidence.
